S1 — Find Your GTM Animal
📅 Thu Jun 12, 2026 · CTA: ruttens.com/quiz · ~900 words
The 6 B2B revenue archetypes — and the exact ceiling each one is about to hit. 🐆 Cheetah · 🦝 Raccoon · 🦓 Zebra · 🐘 Elephant · 🦬 Bison · 🐅 Leopard.
→ ruttens.com/quiz
PIECE 1 — Find Your GTM Animal The 6 B2B revenue archetypes — and the exact ceiling each one is about to hit Primary CTA: Take the quiz → ruttens.com/quiz Every B2B company has a go-to-market engine. Or the conspicuous absence of one. Most leaders can feel which they have. What they can't do is name it — and you can't fix what you can't name. So over 25 years of building revenue engines across more than 40 teams, I started noticing the same six patterns repeating. Different industries, different sizes, same shape. I gave them animals, because animals are easier to remember than frameworks. Here's the thing nobody tells you: each archetype works. Until it doesn't. Every one of them has a ceiling baked in — a point where the very thing that got you here stops you from going further. The trap isn't being the wrong animal. The trap is hitting your ceiling without realising that's what's happening, and responding by doing more of what already stopped working. 🐆 The Cheetah runs on hustle. Founder-led, fast, lethal in a deal. It's working — for now. The ceiling: pipeline is invisible until deals close, and you can't clone yourself. Growth is capped at your personal bandwidth. 🦝 The Raccoon is sharper than 90% of the market. Clever positioning, clever tactics, clever everything. The cleverness is the constraint. The system lives in your head, so every hire "doesn't get it" and fails. You've made yourself the bottleneck. 🦓 The Zebra has the team and the instincts. Some months work. Others you genuinely can't say why. The ceiling: sales and marketing aren't running on the same data, so you're guessing at what's actually driving results — and you can't repeat what you can't see. 🐘 The Elephant is respected and trusted. Everyone who knows you, buys. The problem is everyone who doesn't know you yet doesn't know you exist. Without warm intros, new-business pipeline is thin. Your reputation doesn't scale past your network. 🦬 The Bison won the category and then stopped hunting. Pipeline maintenance quietly replaced pipeline creation. The numbers look stable right up until the trajectory flattens — and flat, in a market that's moving, is slow decline. 🐅 The Leopard cracked the code: precision targeting, clean motion, repeatable results. Only about 1 in 7 B2B teams score here. The challenge isn't fixing anything — it's scaling the precision without losing the thing that made it work. Read those again and one will sting more than the others. That's the useful signal. Knowing your animal does three things. It tells you the specific ceiling you're walking toward, so you stop being surprised by it. It tells you which moves matter for your stage — instead of importing advice built for a company three sizes away from yours. And it gives your team shared language: "we're being a Cheetah about this" is a faster, more honest conversation than a quarterly strategy offsite. I built a 12-question quiz that takes about two minutes and asks for no email. It returns your archetype, the ceiling you're about to hit, and three moves specific to where you are. You'll also get an 8-page playbook for your animal. It's free because the diagnosis should be. What you do next is the part that's worth paying for. Find your GTM Animal → ruttens.com/quiz
S2 — Your €60K on Marketing Should Make €600K in Pipeline
📅 Thu Jun 26, 2026 · CTA: ruttens.com/marketing-roi-fix-sheet · ~1,000 words
Why it doesn't, the three hidden drains, and the 30-day fix. Drain 1: agency activity that isn't pipeline. Drain 2: no attribution. Drain 3: content that doesn't move buyers.
→ ruttens.com/marketing-roi-fix-sheet
PIECE 2 — Your €60K on Marketing Should Make €600K in Pipeline Why it doesn't, the three hidden drains, and the 30-day fix Primary CTA: Get the Fix Sheet → ruttens.com/marketing-roi-fix-sheet Let's start with an uncomfortable number. If you spend €60,000 a year on marketing — a modest budget for a B2B company doing a few million in revenue — that money should be generating at least €600,000 in pipeline. A 10x marketing-to-pipeline ratio isn't aggressive. It's the floor for a functioning engine. Most companies I scan can't tell me their ratio at all. They pay €5–20K a month, they see activity, and they cannot tie a single euro of that spend to a closed deal. The money isn't working. It's leaking. And it leaks in three predictable places. Drain one: agency activity that isn't pipeline. Your agency sends a monthly report full of impressions, reach, engagement, posts shipped. All real. None of it is pipeline. Activity got measured because activity is easy to measure. The output that matters — qualified conversations with buyers — never made it onto the dashboard, because nobody built the dashboard to track it. I've sat with founders paying €4K a month for a content engine whose pipeline impact, measured honestly, was zero. Drain two: no attribution, so you can't kill what's failing. When you can't see which channel produced which conversation, you can't cut the losers. So you keep funding all of them, including the bottom 50% that contribute almost nothing. Spread thin across everything, you're under-investing in the few channels that actually work. Drain three: content that doesn't move buyers. Likes are not intent. A post can perform beautifully on the platform and do nothing for the pipeline, because it spoke to peers instead of buyers, or it described what you do instead of the outcome the buyer gets. Volume without buyer-relevance is just noise you paid for. Here's the part that surprises people: fixing this is mostly subtraction, and it's fast. Kill the bottom 50% of your channels. Not pause — kill. Re-budget toward the top 20%, because that's where roughly 80% of your pipeline already comes from; you're just starving it. Then add one buyer-outcome metric to every case study and every campaign — €X in pipeline, X% lift, X weeks — so that everything you publish is forced to point at a result a buyer cares about. Audit. Kill. Rebuild. Measure. Run that loop over 30 days and the typical result is a 30–60% lift in your marketing-to-pipeline ratio. No new headcount. No new agency. Often a smaller budget, spent with intent. I put the three drains, the nine specific fixes, and the 30-day plan on a single page you can print and hand to your team. No call required. No email gate. The point is to be useful before you ever talk to me. Get the Marketing ROI Fix Sheet → ruttens.com/marketing-roi-fix-sheet And if you'd rather have someone find your specific leaks with you, the Revenue Scan does exactly that in 20 minutes — ruttens.com/revenue-scan.
S3 — Hire a CMO, Hire a Fractional, or Boost In-House?
📅 Thu Jul 10, 2026 · CTA: ruttens.com/team-upgrade-30-day-plan · ~1,100 words
The €180K decision most B2B leaders get wrong. Three states: A (foundational gap → fractional), B (mature but stuck → 60-90d), C (one specific gap → 30d). Only State A points near a CMO.
→ ruttens.com/team-upgrade-30-day-plan
PIECE 3 — Hire a CMO, Hire a Fractional, or Boost In-House? The €180K decision most B2B leaders get wrong Primary CTA: Get the 30-Day Decision Plan → ruttens.com/team-upgrade-30-day-plan The instinct, when marketing is underperforming, is to hire a CMO. It feels decisive. It looks like leadership. And for a meaningful share of companies, it's a €100,000 mistake. Here's the math nobody runs before the job posting goes live. A B2B CMO costs €120–180K a year in salary. Add benefits, add recruiting fees, add the six months it takes a senior hire to learn your business before they produce anything. You're committing to the better part of €200K and half a year before you know whether it was the right call. For a company that needed direction — not a permanent executive — that's an overspend you feel for years. The reason this goes wrong so often is that "our marketing isn't working" gets treated as one problem when it's actually three different ones, each with a different fix. State A: the foundational gap. You have people doing marketing, but no one has ever built the system underneath them. No ICP clarity, no positioning, no engine. A junior team here will stay busy and go nowhere, not because they're weak but because no one's leading. This is the case where fractional leadership earns its keep — six months of a senior operator setting the strategy and building the engine, typically €2,499–€3,990 a month. A fraction of the full-time cost, available now, gone when the engine runs. State B: mature but stuck. The team is capable and the basics exist, but you've plateaued and can't see why. This usually isn't a leadership gap — it's a specific diagnostic-and-rebuild problem. A focused 60–90 day engagement, €1,995–€7,990 fixed, finds the constraint and fixes it. Hiring a CMO here would be using a permanent solution for a temporary problem. State C: one specific gap. Everything works except one thing — your outbound is dead, or your attribution is broken, or your messaging doesn't land. You don't need a CMO or a six-month engagement. You need a 30-day intervention on the one broken part, €1,995–€3,500 fixed. Notice that only State A points anywhere near a CMO-shaped solution — and even then, fractional first. The full-time hire makes sense once the engine exists and you genuinely need a permanent leader to run and scale it. Hiring before that is paying executive prices for work that isn't executive-level yet. So the real question isn't "should we hire a CMO." It's "which of these three states are we actually in." Get that right and the spending decision answers itself. I built a one-page decision plan with a five-question decision tree and a 30-day plan for each path. It's the same logic three RUTTENS+ clients are running right now. Get the Team Upgrade 30-Day Plan → ruttens.com/team-upgrade-30-day-plan
